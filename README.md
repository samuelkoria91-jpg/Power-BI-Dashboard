## Page 1 High Market Overview

![Data Job Dashboard](/Images/Image%201.PNG)


Here are the four headline takeaways from the dashboard data: The market is large but uneven — 479K postings sounds vast, but volume is heavily concentrated in just three roles: Data Analyst, Data Engineer, and Data Scientist account for the bulk of Q1's 10,076 postings. Every other title is a fraction of that. There's a clear pay-volume trade-off the roles with the most job openings (Data Analyst) pay the least ($90K), while the highest-paying roles (ML Engineer, Software Engineer at $155K+) have very few openings. This tells a job seeker: if you want volume, target Analyst roles; if you want pay, specialize. Hiring has a sharp seasonal pattern February peaks and November crashes, which has real implications for when to apply or recruit. Seniority consistently adds 20–25% in salary across every role, the senior variant earns meaningfully more, making career progression a reliable lever for income growth in this field.

## Page 2 High Market Overview

![Data job Dashboard](/Images/Project%201%20Pg%202.PNG)

This is the drill-through page for the Senior Data Scientist role — the most detailed view in your dashboard. Four things stand out immediately: This is a premium, skills-first role. At $156K median and 92.8% of postings requiring no formal degree, it's both high-paying and accessible without a traditional academic path — what matters is demonstrable skill. Remote work is almost guaranteed. Nearly 87% of roles are WFH-eligible, which is unusually high even within the data industry. This makes it one of the most location-flexible senior roles available. LinkedIn is non-negotiable for job seekers. The platform gap between LinkedIn and everything else is stark if someone is targeting this role, LinkedIn is where the market lives. Global demand exists but the US dominates. The world map confirms international postings across Europe and Asia, but the US cluster dwarfs all others, suggesting compensation figures are heavily US-benchmarked.
